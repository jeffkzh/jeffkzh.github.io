How to maximise it

  • Deposit exactly RM5,000, not more. The reward is flat, so every extra ringgit you lock up lowers your effective return. RM5,000 is the sweet spot.
  • Don't let the cash sit at 0%. Moving the RM5,000 into Moneybull earns a money-market yield (~3–4% p.a.) on top of the RM300 while it's parked — roughly RM28–34 extra over 60 days. Understand it's a fund, not a bank deposit (see risks).
  • Never dip below the threshold. No withdrawals, and net deposit must stay at or above RM5,000 for the full 60 days — one early withdrawal disqualifies you from the whole reward.
  • Keep the qualifying capital out of volatile stocks if your goal is the low-risk reward. Use the zero-commission trading perk separately, with money you're genuinely willing to put at risk.
  • Diarise day 60. After it clears, reassess — withdraw, keep investing, or roll into something else.
  • Check for stackable partner promos (e.g. TnG / Boost campaigns) that sometimes run alongside — only if you're eligible and the conditions don't conflict.

Done this way, you collect the RM300 reward plus a bit of fund yield, without ever touching a volatile position.

Turning the RM300 voucher into cash

If the reward arrives as a trading voucher that needs a few trades to unlock rather than as straight cash, the usual approach is to complete the required trades (say 3), then sell the positions the same day — leaving you the cash value minus a little friction. Keep that friction small:

  • Use a liquid Bursa counter, not a US stock. Bursa costs are tiny — 0% commission, 0.03% clearing fee, 0.1% stamp duty (capped), plus 6% SST on the clearing fee. US trades pull in a ~0.5–1% currency-conversion spread each way, which is the real cost to avoid.
  • Mind the spread and intraday moves. Between buying and selling you still carry the bid-ask spread and any price movement — pick a liquid, tight-spread counter and don't leave it sitting.
  • Confirm the exact voucher terms first: how many trades, any minimum size, whether there's a holding period, and the expiry date. Then stay inside the rules — most promos carry an "abuse of system" clause, so keep it to genuinely meeting the trade requirement.

Framed properly, this isn't a loophole — it's meeting the reward's trade condition and then returning to cash.

The risks and fine print

This is genuinely attractive for the effort, but it is not "risk-free" and the RM300 isn't unconditionally "free." Read these honestly:

  • Your capital is tied up. RM5,000 is committed for 60 days with no withdrawals. That's liquidity and opportunity cost, even if the money itself is safe.
  • The reward's form matters. If the RM300 is a trading voucher rather than cash, it may only be usable for trading and could expire — confirm before assuming it's RM300 in your pocket.
  • Moneybull is a fund, not a deposit. It invests in a money-market fund — low risk, but not capital-guaranteed and not PIDM-insured, and the yield isn't fixed. Left as plain cash it's safe but earns nothing.
  • Stocks are where real losses live. If you put the RM5,000 into shares to "use" it, you take full market risk and could lose far more than RM300. The low-risk version keeps the qualifying capital in cash or the money-market fund.
  • Qualification is strict. Miss the threshold, withdraw early, or let net deposit drop, and you forfeit the reward.
  • The reassuring part: Webull Securities (Malaysia) is SC-licensed (CMSL) and a Bursa member, and client funds are held in trust accounts separate from the firm's own money.
